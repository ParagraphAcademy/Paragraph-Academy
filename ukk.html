Popular courses & degree structure

The UK offers a wide range of programs: undergraduates (usually 3–4 years), integrated masters (4 years), taught master's (often 1 year), research degrees (MPhil/PhD), and professional qualifications.

Undergraduate (UG)

Most bachelor's degrees are 3 years (England, Wales, Northern Ireland) or 4 years (Scotland). Many programs include placements, year-abroad options, or industrial experience.

Postgraduate taught (PGT)

Taught master's are frequently 1 year long — intensive but time-efficient. Typical qualifications: MSc, MA, LLM, MBA.

Research degrees

PhD typically 3–4 years full-time. Research degrees require proposals, supervisors, and often funding applications.

Short courses & professional training

Short certificates, diplomas, and professional courses are available across many institutions and private providers.

Entry requirements

  • Academic transcripts and predicted grades (UG via UCAS)
  • Proof of English (IELTS, TOEFL, or equivalent) unless exempt
  • Personal statement or statement of purpose
  • References — usually 1–2 academic referees
  • CV and portfolio for creative/art courses

English test tip

Many universities accept IELTS Academic, TOEFL iBT, PTE Academic, and sometimes Duolingo English Test. Check each program's required scores.

Tuition fees & funding

Tuition varies widely by level, institution, and subject. As an international student, you should plan carefully and check exact fees on each university website.

Ballpark figures

  • Undergraduate (per year): £12,000 — £35,000+
  • Postgraduate taught (per year): £12,000 — £40,000+
  • Professional programs (e.g., MBA): £20,000 — £70,000+

Scholarships & funding

Scholarships include university-specific awards, Commonwealth scholarships, Chevening (for eligible countries), departmental studentships, and external trusts. Early applications and strong supporting documents increase chances.

Chevening

Ambitious mid-career professionals from eligible countries — covers tuition, living allowance.

Commonwealth Scholarships

For students from Commonwealth countries; often merit-based with generous support.

Application process

The application path differs between undergraduate and postgraduate programs.

Undergraduate — UCAS

Most UK undergraduate applications are submitted through UCAS. You can apply to up to 5 courses, submit personal statement, references, and predicted grades.

Postgraduate

For master's and PhDs, many applicants apply directly through the university portal. You'll need references, a statement of purpose, transcripts, and proof of funds.

Interviews & auditions

Some programs (medicine, art, music) require interviews, auditions, or portfolio reviews — prepare early and practice with mentors.

Student visa & immigration basics

The UK Student Route visa allows international students to study in the UK for the duration of their course. Requirements include a CAS (Confirmation of Acceptance for Studies), proof of funds, and English competency.

Key steps

  • Receive an unconditional or conditional offer with CAS from your university
  • Show required funds to cover tuition and living costs (amount depends on location and course length)
  • Apply for the Student Route visa online and attend a biometrics appointment
  • Purchase health surcharge (IHS) as part of the visa application

Work while studying

Full-time students can often work up to 20 hours per week during term (check your visa conditions). Certain courses with placements may permit more work during placement periods.

Proof of funds (example)

You may need to show funds to cover 9 months of living costs plus tuition. London has a higher living requirement than other parts of the UK.

IHS & NHS

As part of your visa you usually pay the immigration health surcharge (IHS) which provides access to NHS services while in the UK.

Living in the UK — costs & accommodation

Cost of living depends heavily on city. London is typically the most expensive; smaller cities and towns can be much cheaper.

Accommodation options

  • University halls: convenient for first year, often with meal plans
  • Private flats and shared houses: common for returning students
  • Homestay: live with a local family — useful for shorter programs

Monthly budget (example)

  • London: £1,200 — £1,800
  • Other cities: £700 — £1,100

Student life, culture & support

The UK is diverse — you'll meet people from many countries and backgrounds. Universities have student unions, clubs, and societies for sports, arts, and academic interests.

Support services

  • International student offices
  • Mental health & counselling
  • Academic support & study skills
  • Career services and placement help

Travel & leisure

The UK is compact — train travel makes short trips easy. Enjoy festivals, museums, pubs, theatres, and national parks in your free time.

Working, internships & post-study options

Work experience is valuable. Many students take internships, part-time roles, and placements to build skills and networks.

Post-study Work (Graduate route)

After successful completion of eligible courses, international students may apply for the Graduate route to stay and work in the UK for 2 years (3 years for doctoral graduates).

Job search tips

  • Use university career portals and job fairs
  • Build a UK-style CV and LinkedIn profile
  • Network with alumni and local companies

Practical tips for applicants

  • Start early — top programs are competitive
  • Tailor your personal statement to each course
  • Get strong references; brief your referees on deadlines
  • Prepare proof of funds and documents for visa interviews
  • Consider short courses to test UK study style before committing

Frequently asked questions

How long does a master's degree take?
Taught master's are often 1 year full-time; research master's and MRes programs can take longer.
Can I work while studying?
Yes — subject to visa restrictions (commonly up to 20 hours/week during term time).
Is living more expensive in London?
Yes — London living costs are typically higher than most other UK cities.
Do I need a student visa?
Yes, non-UK/EEA students usually need a Student route visa. See university guidance and official immigration pages for exact rules.
